# Political Education

> **Status:** starter directory. The plan calls for a full political
> education module as Agent 9 task 4 — short enough to be accessible,
> deep enough to actually inform practice. This README is the
> scaffolding.

Understoria is software, but it is not neutral software. It encodes
a set of beliefs — about labor, reciprocity, power, and trust — that
came from real movements and real struggles. This directory is for
reading, discussion questions, and resources so communities using
Understoria can locate themselves in that history.

---

## What to study first

If you have time for only **one thing**, read Dean Spade's short
essay introducing mutual aid in the 2020 moment:

- Dean Spade, *Solidarity Not Charity* (2020). The essay is
  available in long and short forms across several publications;
  the 2020 *Social Text* version is the canonical one.

If you have time for **a book**, pick one:

- Peter Kropotkin, *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution* (1902).
  Foundational. Kropotkin observes mutual aid in animals and in
  pre-industrial human societies and argues it's a survival
  strategy, not an exception to self-interest.
- Dean Spade, *Mutual Aid: Building Solidarity During This Crisis
  (and the Next)* (2020). Applied, contemporary, short.

## What Understoria draws from

### Mutual aid tradition

- Black Panther Party survival programs (free breakfast, free
  clinics, sickle-cell screening).
- Young Lords' community health programs.
- Zapatista caracoles and community self-governance.
- Disability justice frameworks — Mia Mingus, Leah Lakshmi
  Piepzna-Samarasinha — on interdependence as a precondition,
  not a fallback.
- COVID-era mutual aid networks that emerged spontaneously in
  thousands of communities worldwide in 2020 and many of which
  persist.

### Timebanking

- Edgar Cahn, *No More Throw-Away People: The Co-Production
  Imperative* (2000). Cahn coined modern timebanking and, crucially,
  insisted on the equal-hour principle — an hour of anyone's time
  equals an hour of anyone else's — because markets price labor in
  ways that devalue caregiving and community work. Understoria
  inherits this directly.
- TimeBanks USA's practical guides to operating a time bank.

### Labor organizing

- Jane McAlevey, *No Shortcuts: Organizing for Power in the New
  Gilded Age* (2016). On deep organizing versus shallow mobilizing.
- Kim Kelly, *Fight Like Hell: The Untold History of American
  Labor* (2022). A panorama that connects mutual aid practice to
  union history.
- *Labor Notes* (ongoing). The best ongoing coverage of rank-and-
  file organizing practice.

### Cooperatives and horizontal governance

- Jo Freeman, *The Tyranny of Structurelessness* (1972). The
  essential critique of informal hierarchy. Understoria's
  governance model is built around Freeman's insight that
  structure is happening whether you name it or not.
- Sociocracy and modified consensus literature — Sociocracy for
  All, the Seeds for Change guides.

### Digital commons and privacy

- Shoshana Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019).
  Long, dense, worth it.
- EFF Surveillance Self-Defense — <https://ssd.eff.org>.
- Cory Doctorow, *The Internet Con* (2023), on interoperability
  and why federated systems matter.

## Discussion prompts for study groups

### On the platform itself

1. What did timebanks and mutual aid networks do before there was
   software for it? What did they lose when software arrived, and
   what did they gain? Where should Understoria sit in that
   tradeoff?
2. Understoria's design principle is one-hour-equals-one-hour.
   What work is that principle protecting? What critiques does it
   invite? Are there cases in your community where it gets in the
   way?
3. If we removed the app tomorrow, what would we still have? That
   answer is the actual foundation; the app is scaffolding.

### On mutual aid and charity

4. Dean Spade distinguishes mutual aid from charity by who gets to
   decide. Who makes decisions in your community right now? Who
   doesn't?
5. Mutual aid projects often get absorbed by NGOs or turned into
   service-provision programs. What protects your community from
   that pull?
6. Who in your community isn't asking for help even though they
   need it? Why?

### On organizing

7. McAlevey distinguishes mobilizing (getting existing supporters
   to show up) from organizing (winning over people who aren't
   yet supporters). Is your mutual aid network a mobilizing
   project, an organizing one, or both?
8. Mutual aid work and union work have historically fed each
   other. Where are the connections in your context? What's
   possible that isn't being tried?

### On power and governance

9. Freeman argues that pretending to be structureless doesn't make
   you structureless; it just makes the structure informal and
   harder to challenge. What informal structures exist in your
   community? Are they working?
10. If Understoria's software decisions were being made by a
    corporation rather than a cooperative, what would be different
    about its features? Write down three.

## Workshop outline (90 minutes)

For communities onboarding Understoria:

- **(10 min)** Open with a round. "A time someone helped me that I
  didn't forget."
- **(20 min)** Read Dean Spade's short essay or the Kropotkin
  preface together. Silent reading, then discussion.
- **(20 min)** Introduce the app. Walk through the member guide's
  post → claim → confirm flow on a phone, not a projector.
- **(20 min)** Discussion prompts 1, 2, and 3 from above.
- **(10 min)** Logistics for first week (buddy pairings, first
  meeting schedule).
- **(10 min)** Closing round. "One thing I'm going to try this
  week."

## A note on voice

The readings above center Dean Spade's and Kropotkin's framings
because they're the most widely available. That doesn't mean they
are the only lineages worth learning from. Black radical,
Indigenous, disability-justice, and feminist frameworks have been
doing mutual aid theory for much longer than it's been called that
in English-language academia. When you add to this list, center
those voices where you can.
